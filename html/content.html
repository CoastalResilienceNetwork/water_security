Documentation

This app enables users to explore how cities could improve the quality of their intake water by implementing conservation activities to reduce nonpoint pollution. The app depicts how forest protection, pastureland reforestation, and agricultural best-management practices (BMPs) can reduce sediment and phosphorus pollution for surface water intakes for 469 cities.

Most cities use water from multiple surface water sources. The data presented here represent average values across all source watersheds for a given city. Additionally, while many cities also rely upon groundwater sources, this app focuses only on surface water supplies. For more details on methodology, please refer to the data documentation provided on this site.

Note that the cities mapped here include only those with explicitly surveyed water sources. In the report, Beyond the Source, we additionally model potential surface water sources for more than 3,500 additional cities.

Pollutant Load in Source Watersheds

Nonpoint pollution includes a wide array of pollutant types but two pollutants have particular relevance for urban source water protection: sediment and nutrients.

In practice, phosphorus and nitrogen loading are highly spatially correlated at large scales and the phosphorus results presented here are indicative of similar global spatial patterns for nitrogen loading.

Pollution Reduction Potential

We consider three conservation practices relevant to source water protection: forest protection, pastureland reforestation and agricultural BMPs. For a given watershed, we estimate the area and cost of the optimal portfolio of conservation activities needed to reach a given reduction target for sediment or phosphorus (10%, 20%, or 30% reduction relative to baseline conditions).
